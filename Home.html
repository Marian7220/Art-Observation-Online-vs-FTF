Analyzing Art Observation – Online vs FaceToFace

Report by Yaseen Abd Elhaleem, Michael Mogrman and Marian Abu Saleh
Computational Human Behavior Lab 2022

The Problem

The goal is to compare between the way people observe art in museum (where they can see the painting physically) and the way they observe it on screen.

______________________________________________________________________________________________________________________________

Our Approach

Our plan was to go with the participants to the museum and film them while they look at 5 different paintings for the first time and then after a while (2 weeks or more) we'd invite the participants again and film them while looking at the same paintings on a compputer screen.
After we gather all needed data we'd filter it, analyze it and represent it in different forms (heatmaps, clusters of gazes, etc..) so that we could get results.

A Brief Timeline Of Work

- Choose the art piecies which our participants will look at (5 paintings were chosen).
- Gather participants.
- Film with participants at the museum.
- Film with participants on computer screen (2 weeks later).
- Filter the data we got and trying to represent it as informative as we could.
- Concluding results from the data.

A Technical Point Of View

We used the Pupil Core eye tracker to gather data of where each subject was looking at the painting, we had a protocol to work by for filming:

  1. Gather details of the participants (including stuff that would help to conclude results, like: "do you paint?")
  2. Calibrate the eye tracker (for each participant and each one of the 5 paintings)
  3. Reveal the painting and start filming (we hid the paintings in advance so that we could record the first seconds of observing the art piece)

Afterwards, after we gathered all data, we worked on filtering it and representing in in different forms which are:

  1. Heat-Maps.
  2. Gaze Clusters And Transitions.
  3. Scan Paths.

Brief Explanation And Examples

Heat-Maps:

A heat-map is a data visualization that shows the number of times the participant looked at a specific area in the picture. It shows that by color - red is the highest magnitude and blue is the lowest (meaning that red areas are the ones where the participant looked at hte most and blue areas are the leastt).

Scan-Paths:

In this representation of the data we show the points the participant focused on for a while and the transitions between these points.
The cyan aura is where they looked at first, then you go according to the arrows, the scale of the aura indicates the duration of looking at that point.

Gaze Clusters And Transitions:

Clusters:  k-means clustering is a method of vector quantization, that aims to partition n observations into k clusters in which each observation belongs to the cluster with the nearest mean. For each image we presented the k-means clustering for 30 seconds.
Cluster Histogram:  shows the size of the observation in each area.
Transitions Matrix:  The cell [i, j] in the matrix contains the number of times the participants looked at zone i and then moved straight to zone j, the center of the clusters are the regions we want to see how many times the participant passed her gaze between.

Also we show the transition between areas in arrows, the size of arrows symbolizes how many times the participant moved from one area to another.
